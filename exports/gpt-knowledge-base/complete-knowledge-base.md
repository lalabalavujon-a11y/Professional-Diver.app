## Underwater Welding Fundamentals

### Wet Welding vs. Dry Welding
Understanding the two primary underwater welding methods:

#### Wet Welding (Hyperbaric Welding)
- **Process**: Direct arc welding in water environment
- **Advantages**: Faster setup, lower cost, greater mobility
- **Limitations**: Reduced arc stability, higher porosity risk
- **Applications**: Emergency repairs, temporary fixes, non-critical welds

#### Dry Welding (Habitat Welding)
- **Process**: Welding in pressurized dry environment
- **Advantages**: Superior weld quality, better control
- **Limitations**: Complex setup, higher cost, limited access
- **Applications**: Critical structural welds, permanent repairs

### Electrochemical Principles
Understanding the science behind underwater welding:

#### Arc Stability in Water
- **Gas Bubble Formation**: Hydrogen and oxygen bubble creation
- **Arc Column**: Electrical path through gas bubbles
- **Voltage Requirements**: Higher voltage needed for underwater conditions
- **Current Density**: Increased current requirements for penetration

#### Metal Transfer Mechanisms
- **Globular Transfer**: Large droplet formation in water
- **Spray Transfer**: Fine droplet transfer in dry conditions
- **Short Circuit Transfer**: Low voltage, high current transfer
- **Pulsed Transfer**: Controlled droplet transfer for quality

## Electrode Selection and Management

### Electrode Types for Underwater Welding

#### E6013 Electrodes
- **Characteristics**: All-position, easy striking
- **Applications**: General purpose underwater welding
- **Advantages**: Good arc stability, minimal spatter
- **Limitations**: Lower tensile strength, higher porosity

#### E7018 Electrodes
- **Characteristics**: Low hydrogen, high strength
- **Applications**: Critical structural welds
- **Advantages**: Superior mechanical properties
- **Limitations**: Requires dry storage, sensitive to moisture

#### Specialized Underwater Electrodes
- **Waterproof Coating**: Moisture-resistant flux coating
- **Extended Length**: Longer electrodes for underwater use
- **Enhanced Flux**: Improved gas bubble formation
- **Quality Control**: Strict storage and handling requirements

### Electrode Storage and Handling
Critical procedures for electrode management:

#### Storage Requirements
- **Temperature Control**: 70-80°F (21-27°C) storage temperature
- **Humidity Control**: Maximum 50% relative humidity
- **Container Sealing**: Airtight containers with desiccant
- **Rotation System**: First-in, first-out inventory management

#### Pre-Use Preparation
- **Visual Inspection**: Check for coating damage or moisture
- **Baking Procedure**: 250°F (121°C) for 2 hours if needed
- **Immediate Use**: Use within 4 hours of removal from storage
- **Waste Management**: Proper disposal of unused electrodes

#### Safety Equipment Requirements
- **Insulated Tools**: Non-conductive hand tools and equipment
- **Ground Fault Interrupters**: GFCI protection on all circuits
- **Emergency Shutdown**: Quick-disconnect systems for power
- **Personal Protection**: Insulated gloves and protective clothing

#### Emergency Procedures

- **Bubble Size**: Large bubbles affecting visibility and stability

### Carbon Steel Welding

### Stainless Steel Welding

- **Interpass Temperature**: Maximum 300°F (149°C)
- **Post-Weld Cleaning**: Passivation treatment required

#### Duplex Stainless Steel (2205, 2507)
- **Electrode Selection**: E2209 or E2594 electrodes
- **Heat Input Control**: Precise control for phase balance
- **Cooling Rate**: Controlled cooling for proper microstructure
- **Testing Requirements**: Ferrite content verification

## Fundamental Gas Laws

### Boyle's Law
**Principle**: At constant temperature, the volume of a gas is inversely proportional to its pressure.

**Formula**: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

**Diving Applications**:
- **Lung Volume**: At 33 feet (2 ATA), lung volume is halved
- **Buoyancy Control**: BCD inflation requirements change with depth
- **Equipment Design**: Pressure vessel calculations for tanks and chambers

**Practical Example**: A diver's lungs contain 6 liters at the surface. At 33 feet (2 ATA), the same air occupies only 3 liters.

### Charles's Law
**Principle**: At constant pressure, the volume of a gas is directly proportional to its temperature.

**Formula**: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂

**Diving Applications**:
- **Thermal Expansion**: Gas volume changes with temperature
- **Equipment Performance**: Regulator performance in cold water
- **Safety Considerations**: Temperature effects on gas calculations

### Dalton's Law
**Principle**: The total pressure of a gas mixture is the sum of the partial pressures of its components.

**Formula**: P_total = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ...

**Diving Applications**:
- **Nitrogen Narcosis**: Partial pressure of nitrogen affects mental function
- **Oxygen Toxicity**: Partial pressure of oxygen determines toxicity risk
- **Gas Mixtures**: Calculating safe breathing gas compositions

## Pressure Effects on the Human Body

### Barotrauma
**Definition**: Tissue damage caused by pressure differences between body cavities and the environment.

**Types of Barotrauma**:
- **Ear Barotrauma**: Middle ear pressure equalization problems
- **Sinus Barotrauma**: Sinus pressure equalization issues
- **Pulmonary Barotrauma**: Lung overexpansion injuries
- **Gastrointestinal Barotrauma**: Stomach and intestinal gas expansion

**Prevention**:
- Proper equalization techniques
- Gradual descent rates
- Avoiding diving with congestion
- Proper ascent rates

### Nitrogen Narcosis
**Definition**: Reversible alteration of consciousness caused by high partial pressure of nitrogen.

**Symptoms**:
- Euphoria and overconfidence
- Impaired judgment and coordination
- Slowed reaction times
- Memory problems

**Management**:
- Recognizing early symptoms
- Ascending to shallower depths
- Using helium-based gas mixtures for deep dives
- Proper dive planning and buddy awareness

## Decompression Theory

### Nitrogen Absorption
**Process**: Nitrogen dissolves in body tissues during exposure to increased pressure.

**Factors Affecting Absorption**:
- **Depth**: Deeper dives increase nitrogen loading
- **Time**: Longer bottom times increase nitrogen absorption
- **Tissue Type**: Different tissues absorb nitrogen at different rates
- **Exercise**: Physical activity affects circulation and absorption

### Decompression Schedules
**No-Decompression Limits**: Maximum bottom times that allow direct ascent to surface.

**Decompression Stops**: Required stops at specific depths to allow nitrogen elimination.

**Safety Factors**:
- Conservative dive planning
- Proper ascent rates
- Emergency decompression procedures
- Surface interval requirements

## Gas Calculations

### Air Consumption Rates
**Surface Air Consumption (SAC)**: Air consumption rate at the surface.

**Formula**: SAC = (Tank Pressure Used × Tank Volume) / (Bottom Time × Average Depth)

**Applications**:
- Dive planning and gas requirements
- Emergency gas sharing calculations
- Equipment selection and sizing

### Gas Mixing
**Partial Pressure Method**: Calculating gas mixture compositions.

**Formula**: P_partial = P_total × Fraction

**Applications**:
- Nitrox mixture preparation
- Trimix calculations
- Oxygen toxicity management

## Safety Applications

### Dive Planning
- **Maximum Operating Depth**: Calculating safe depth limits
- **Bottom Time Limits**: Determining safe exposure times
- **Gas Requirements**: Calculating total gas needs
- **Emergency Procedures**: Planning for equipment failures

### Emergency Response

- **Gas Sharing**: Calculating emergency gas requirements
- **Decompression Illness**: Recognizing and treating DCI
- **Equipment Failures**: Managing pressure-related problems

## The ABCDE Approach

### A - Airway
- **Assessment**: Check for airway obstruction and breathing
- **Management**: Clear airway, position patient, consider advanced airway
- **Documentation**: Record airway status and interventions

### B - Breathing
- **Assessment**: Evaluate respiratory rate, effort, and oxygen saturation
- **Management**: Provide oxygen, assist ventilation if needed
- **Monitoring**: Continuous respiratory assessment

### C - Circulation
- **Assessment**: Check pulse, blood pressure, and perfusion
- **Management**: Control bleeding, maintain circulation
- **Fluid Management**: IV access and fluid resuscitation

### D - Disability
- **Assessment**: Neurological evaluation using AVPU scale
- **Management**: Protect spine, monitor consciousness
- **Documentation**: Record neurological status

### E - Exposure/Environment
- **Assessment**: Full body examination for injuries
- **Management**: Maintain body temperature, treat hypothermia
- **Safety**: Ensure safe environment for patient and rescuers

- Always follow ABCDE sequence
- Reassess continuously
- Document all findings and interventions
- Communicate clearly with medical team

## Saturation Diving Fundamentals

### What is Saturation Diving?
**Definition**: A diving technique that allows divers to work at great depths for extended periods by maintaining them at the working pressure.

**Key Principles**:
- **Pressure Equalization**: Divers live at working depth pressure
- **No Decompression**: No daily decompression required
- **Extended Bottom Time**: Unlimited working time at depth
- **Single Decompression**: One decompression at end of mission

**Advantages**:
- Unlimited bottom time at working depth
- Cost-effective for long-duration projects
- Reduced decompression risk
- Improved work efficiency

# CSWIP 3.1U Underwater Inspection - General Visual Inspection (GVI) and Close Visual Inspection (CVI)

## Terminology

- **GVI (General Visual Inspection)**: Broad overview inspection covering large areas to identify obvious defects, structural issues, and areas requiring closer examination
- **CVI (Close Visual Inspection)**: Detailed examination within arm's reach, typically within 600mm, to identify specific defects, cracks, corrosion, and surface conditions
- **Inspection Scope**: Defines what areas to inspect, what defects to look for, and what acceptance criteria apply

## Inspection Procedures

### Pre-Inspection Planning
- Review inspection scope and requirements
- Identify inspection areas and access points
- Prepare inspection equipment and documentation
- Conduct safety briefings and risk assessments

### Visual Inspection Techniques
- Systematic examination patterns (grid, spiral, linear)
- Proper lighting and visibility requirements
- Documentation standards (photos, videos, measurements)
- Defect identification and classification

### Defect Classification
- Critical defects requiring immediate action
- Major defects requiring scheduled repair
- Minor defects for monitoring
- Acceptable conditions within tolerance

## Reporting Requirements

### A-LODER Reporting Format
- **A**: Assessment and findings summary
- **L**: Location and position coordinates
- **O**: Observations and measurements
- **D**: Defect classification and severity
- **E**: Evidence (photos, videos, measurements)
- **R**: Recommendations and required actions

## Industry Standards
